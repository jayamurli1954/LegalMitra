  - Key Sections:

   - **Court**: Supreme Court of India  
   - **Year**: 1995  
   - **Key Principle**: Medical services are covered under the definition of "service" in consumer protection laws, and deficiency in medical services can be challenged before consumer forums.  
   - **Relevance**: Clarifies the scope of "service" under the Act, ensuring consumers can file complaints for professional services.

   - **Bench Strength**: 2-Judge Bench

**LEGAL POSITION SUMMARY**  
Under the Consumer Protection Act, 2019, a consumer (as defined under Section 2(7)) can file a complaint against a seller, manufacturer, or service provider for any defect in goods, deficiency in services, unfair trade practices, or violation of consumer rights. The Act establishes a three-tier redressal mechanism: District Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission (District Commission), State Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission (State Commission), and National Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission (National Commission). The 2019 Act also introduces provisions for e-filing of complaints and mediation as an alternative dispute resolution mechanism. There are no conflicting precedents on the basic procedure for filing complaints, and the law is settled on the jurisdiction and process as outlined in the Act.

---

**PROCESS FOR FILING A CONSUMER COMPLAINT IN INDIA**  

**1. Eligibility to File a Complaint**  
- The complainant must be a "consumer" as defined under Section 2(7) of the Act, i.e., a person who buys goods or avails services for personal use (not for resale or commercial purposes).  
- Complaints can also be filed by recognized consumer associations, legal heirs (in case of death of the consumer), or a group of consumers with the same interest (class action).  

**2. Grounds for Filing a Complaint (Section 2(9) and Sections 35-41)**  
A complaint can be filed for:  
- Defect in goods (Section 2(10))  
- Deficiency in services (Section 2(11))  
- Unfair trade practices (Section 2(47))  
- Charging a price above the marked price or refusing to issue a bill  
- Violation of consumer rights, including the right to safety, information, choice, and redressal  

**3. Jurisdiction of Consumer Commissions**  
- **District Commission**: Handles complaints where the value of goods/services (including compensation claimed) does not exceed ₹50 lakh (Section 34). The complaint must be filed in the district where the opposite party resides or carries on business, or where the cause of action arose.  
- **State Commission**: Handles complaints valued between ₹50 lakh and ₹2 crore (Section 47). It also hears appeals from District Commissions.  
- **National Commission**: Handles complaints valued above ₹2 crore (Section 58). It also hears appeals from State Commissions.  

**4. Step-by-Step Procedure for Filing a Complaint**  
   a. **Drafting the Complaint**:  
      - The complaint must be in writing (or filed online via the e-Daakhil portal).  
      - It should include:  
        - Name, description, and address of the complainant and the opposite party.  
        - Facts of the case, including when and where the cause of action arose.  
        - Details of the defect/deficiency or unfair practice.  
        - Relief sought (e.g., refund, replacement, compensation).  
        - Supporting documents (e.g., bills, receipts, warranty cards, correspondence).  
   b. **Filing the Complaint**:  
      - File the complaint with the appropriate Consumer Commission based on the pecuniary jurisdiction.  
      - Complaints can be filed physically at the Commission’s office or online through the e-Daakhil portal (https://edaakhil.nic.in).  
      - Pay the prescribed court fee (varies based on the value of the claim; nominal fees ranging from ₹200 to ₹7,500 as per the Consumer Protection Rules, 2020). No fee is required if the complainant is below the poverty line.  
   c. **Admission of Complaint**:  
      - The Commission will scrutinize the complaint within 21 days of filing to decide whether to admit or reject it (Section 36).  
      - If admitted, a notice is issued to the opposite party to file a written statement within 30 days (extendable by 15 days).  
   d. **Hearing and Evidence**:  
      - Both parties present their arguments and evidence.  
      - The Commission may refer the matter for mediation under Section 37 if both parties agree.  
   e. **Final Order**:  
      - The Commission must decide the complaint within 3 months from the date of receipt of the opposite party’s written statement (5 months if laboratory testing is involved) as per Section 38(7).  
      - Reliefs may include refund, replacement, compensation for loss or injury, and costs.  
   f. **Appeal**:  
      - An appeal against the District Commission’s order can be filed with the State Commission within 45 days (Section 41).  
      - An appeal against the State Commission’s order can be filed with the National Commission within 30 days (Section 51).  
      - An appeal against the National Commission’s order can be filed with the Supreme Court within 30 days (Section 67).  

**5. Limitation Period**  
- As per Section 69 of the Consumer Protection Act, 2019, a complaint must be filed within 2 years from the date on which the cause of action arose.  
- The Commission may condone delays if sufficient cause is shown.  

**6. E-Filing Option (e-Daakhil Portal)**  
- The e-Daakhil portal, launched by the National Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission (NCDRC), allows consumers to file complaints online.  
- Steps:  
  1. Register on the portal (https://edaakhil.nic.in).  
  2. Fill in the complaint details and upload supporting documents.  
  3. Pay the court fee online.  
  4. Track the status of the complaint online.  

**7. Mediation as an Alternative**  
- Under Section 37, the Consumer Commission may refer the matter to mediation if both parties consent.  
- Mediation centers are attached to Consumer Commissions at all levels.  
- If a settlement is reached, it is recorded and binding. If not, the case returns to the Commission for adjudication.

**INDUSTRY-SPECIFIC COMPLIANCE**  
- **For Consumers**: No specific compliance is required beyond filing the complaint with necessary documents and fees.  
- **For Businesses/Service Providers**:  
  - Ensure compliance with the Consumer Protection Act, 2019, including providing accurate product information, issuing bills/receipts, and honoring warranties.  
  - Respond to consumer complaints within the stipulated time to avoid penalties.  
  - Penalties for non-compliance include fines and compensation orders by the Commission.

**PRACTICAL ADVICE**  
1. **Initial Steps**:  
   - Attempt to resolve the issue directly with the seller/service provider by sending a written complaint or legal notice. Retain copies of all correspondence.  
   - Collect and preserve evidence such as bills, receipts, warranty documents, advertisements, and screenshots (for online transactions).  
2. **Drafting and Filing**:  
   - Use a clear and concise format for the complaint. Templates are available on the NCDRC website or e-Daakhil portal.  
   - File the complaint in the correct jurisdiction based on the value of the claim and location of the opposite party or cause of action.  
3. **Documentation**:  
   - Attach all relevant documents (invoices, contracts, proof of payment, etc.) with the complaint.  
   - Keep originals safe and submit attested copies.  
4. **Timelines**:  
   - Adhere to the 2-year limitation period under Section 69. If delayed, file an application for condonation of delay with valid reasons.  
5. **Forum for Dispute Resolution**:  
   - Choose between physical filing at the Consumer Commission or online filing via e-Daakhil.  
   - Opt for mediation if a quick resolution is preferred and the opposite party agrees.  
6. **Approximate Costs and Fees**:  
   - Court fees for filing complaints (as per Consumer Protection Rules, 2020):  
     - Up to ₹5 lakh: ₹200  
     - ₹5 lakh to ₹10 lakh: ₹400  
     - ₹10 lakh to ₹20 lakh: ₹500  
     - ₹20 lakh to ₹50 lakh: ₹2,000  
     - ₹50 lakh to ₹1 crore: ₹4,000  
     - Above ₹1 crore: ₹5,000 to ₹7,500 (varies by Commission).  
   - Legal fees for hiring an advocate (optional) vary based on the complexity of the case and location (₹5,000 to ₹50,000 or more).  
7. **Risks and Alternatives**:  
   - Risk of complaint rejection if filed in the wrong jurisdiction or beyond the limitation period.  
   - Alternative: Approach the seller for an out-of-court settlement or use the National Consumer Helpline (NCH) at 1915 or via their app/website for guidance before filing a formal complaint.  

---

**CONCLUSION**  
Filing a consumer complaint in India under the Consumer Protection Act, 2019, is a structured process designed to be accessible to individuals. With the introduction of e-filing and mediation, the process has become more consumer-friendly. It is critical to adhere to jurisdictional limits, timelines, and documentation requirements to ensure the complaint is admitted and adjudicated effectively. For complex cases or high-value claims, consulting a legal practitioner specializing in consumer law is advisable to strengthen the case and navigate appeals if necessary.





Response from  Indian Kanoon

The process for filing a consumer complaint in India is governed primarily by the Consumer Protection Act, 2019 (CPA, 2019), which establishes a three-tier quasi-judicial mechanism known as the Consumer Disputes Redressal Agencies (Consumer Commissions).

As "Know your Kanoon," I will outline the essential steps, jurisdiction, and legal principles involved in initiating a consumer complaint.

Process for Filing a Consumer Complaint under CPA, 2019
1. Who Can File a Complaint?
A complaint can be filed by a 'consumer' (as defined under Section 2(7) of the CPA, 2019), which includes:

Any person who buys goods or hires or avails any service for a consideration.
Any recognized consumer association.
One or more consumers having the same interest.
The Central or State Government.
Legal heirs or representatives of the consumer.
2. Grounds for Complaint
A complaint can be filed regarding:

Defect in goods purchased.
Deficiency in service hired or availed.
Charging of a price in excess of the price fixed by law or displayed.
Offering for sale of hazardous goods or services (as noted in the analysis in U.P. Power Corporation Ltd. & Ors vs Anis Ahmad on 1 July, 2013 [Supreme Court of India] (https://indiankanoon.org/doc/55216283/)).
Unfair contracts or unfair trade practices.
3. Determining Jurisdiction
The CPA, 2019 defines the appropriate Consumer Commission based on the pecuniary (monetary) value of the goods or services paid for, plus the compensation claimed (excluding interest and litigation costs):

Forum	Pecuniary Jurisdiction (Value of Goods/Services + Compensation)
District Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission	Up to ₹50 Lakhs
State Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission	Above ₹50 Lakhs up to ₹2 Crores
National Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission (NCDRC)	Above ₹2 Crores
Territorial Jurisdiction: The complaint must be filed where:

The opposite party resides or carries on business.
The cause of action wholly or partly arose.
The complainant resides or personally works for gain (a significant change introduced by the CPA, 2019).
4. Limitation Period
As per Section 69 of the CPA, 2019, a complaint must be filed within two years from the date on which the cause of action arose. The Commission may condone the delay if the complainant satisfies the Commission that there was sufficient cause for the delay.

5. Steps for Filing the Complaint
Drafting the Complaint: The complaint should be in writing and contain:
Name, description, and address of the complainant and the opposite party.
Facts relating to the complaint and when the cause of action arose.
Documents supporting the allegations (e.g., invoices, receipts, correspondence, warranty cards).
The relief sought (e.g., refund, replacement, compensation, removal of defects).
Affidavit and Verification: The complaint must be verified and supported by an affidavit confirming that the facts stated are true and correct.
Filing Fee: A prescribed fee must be paid, which varies based on the value of the claim and the specific Commission (District, State, or National).
Submission: The complaint can be filed either physically at the appropriate Commission or electronically through the E-Daakhil portal (an online platform established under the CPA, 2019).
Admission and Notice: Once filed, the Commission decides on the admissibility of the complaint, usually within 21 days. If admitted, a notice is issued to the opposite party, directing them to file their response (written statement) within 30 days (extendable by 15 days).
6. Key Legal Principles Regarding Maintainability
The remedy provided under the Consumer Protection Act is considered an additional avenue for relief, not a replacement for other laws.

Remedy is Additional (Section 3 of CPA): The Supreme Court has consistently held that the remedies available under the CPA are "in addition to and not in derogation of the provisions of any other law for the time being in force."
In National Seeds Corporation Ltd. Vs. M. Madhusudhan Reddy & Anr. (2012) [Supreme Court] (https://indiankanoon.org/search/?formInput=National+Seeds+Corporation+Ltd.+vs+M.+Madhusudhan+Reddy), the Court clarified that a consumer can choose to file a complaint under the CPA even if other remedies, such as arbitration, are available.
Arbitration Clause is Not a Bar: Following the principle of Section 3, the existence of an arbitration clause in an agreement does not bar the Consumer Commission from entertaining a complaint.
This position was affirmed in Dlf Limited vs Mridul Estate (Pvt.) Ltd on 13 May, 2013 [National Consumer Disputes Redressal] (https://indiankanoon.org/doc/143047388/), which held that the remedy provided under the CPA is in addition to the provisions of any other law, and therefore, an arbitration clause will not bar the entertainment of the complaint.
This was further reiterated in cases like Abha Arora vs Puma Realtors Pvt. Ltd. on 1 April, 2016 [State Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission] (https://indiankanoon.org/doc/112828725/).
Summary: Filing a consumer complaint requires identifying the appropriate Consumer Commission based on pecuniary jurisdiction, drafting a detailed complaint outlining the deficiency or defect, attaching supporting documents, and ensuring the filing is done within the two-year limitation period. The consumer forum provides a specialized, additional remedy for consumers.
